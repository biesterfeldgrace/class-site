Culture

In India, people gather at roadside chai wallahs (vendors) to gossip, socialize, and even strike business deals over steaming cups of chai. Offering chai is a gesture of hospitality in every home, and for many, the day simply doesn't begin without it. It's a daily pause that brings people together, blending conversation, comfort, and community.

Variations

Chai takes on countless forms, changing from region to region, vendor to vendor, even family to family. The most common is Masala Chai, the spiced, milky version most people know, but there are many variations—like Noon Chai, a salty pink tea from Kashmir; Irani Chai, rich with condensed milk in Hyderabad; and Tulsi Chai, infused with holy basil for its healing qualities.

Tea Tea

The word “chai” simply means “tea”—it comes from the Hindi and Urdu chai, which in turn comes from the Chinese word chá. So when people say “chai tea,” they're really saying “tea tea.” What most of us think of as “chai” is actually masala chai: black tea simmered with milk, sugar, and warming spices.

Kulhads

In many parts of India, chai is served in little clay cups called kulhads. These cups are single-use and traditionally smashed after drinking, returning the clay back to the earth. Beyond being eco-friendly, they also give the chai a subtle earthy aroma and flavor that glass or plastic can't match.

A Cup of Tea

by Anonymous

When the world is all at odds
And the mind is all at sea
Then cease the useless tedium
And brew a cup of tea.

There is magic in its fragrance,
There is solace in its taste;
And the laden moments vanish
Somehow into space.

And the world becomes a lovely thing!
There's beauty as you'll see;
All because you briefly stopped
To brew a cup of tea.
